Dodo

The Dodo (*Raphus cucullatus*) is one of the most famous examples of an extinct species. Native to Mauritius, the Dodo was a flightless bird that thrived in its predator-free environment before human intervention.

Weighing about 15–20 kilograms (30–40 pounds) and standing approximately 3 feet tall, the Dodo was well-adapted to its habitat, feeding primarily on fruits, seeds, and roots. With no natural predators, it had no need for the ability to fly.

However, the arrival of humans and introduced animals such as rats, pigs, and monkeys in the 16th century spelled disaster for the Dodo. These animals preyed on Dodo eggs, while humans hunted the birds for food. Within less than a century, the Dodo became extinct, with the last recorded sighting in the late 1600s.

The story of the Dodo serves as a poignant reminder of the impact of human activities on wildlife and ecosystems.
